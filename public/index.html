FAQs

What is PM2.5?

PM2.5 represents particles with a diameter of 2.5 micrometres or less. These particles are so diminutive that they are approximately 30 times smaller than the width of a human strand. PM2.5 particles are capable of being inhaled deeply into the lungs and even entering the bloodstream, which poses significant health dangers, due to their diminutive size.

How does air quality affect health?

Human health is significantly affected by air quality, which has both short-term and long-term consequences. A variety of health issues can result from poor air quality, which is caused by pollutants such as PM2.5, PM10, ozone (O₃), nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), and sulphur dioxide (SO₂).

What are the primary sources of air pollution?

Air pollution is caused by both natural sources like wildfires and dust storms and human activities such as industrial emissions, vehicle exhaust, burning of fossil fuels, and agricultural activities.

What is the AQI (Air Quality Index)?

The AQI is a standardized system used to measure and report air quality levels. It assigns a value based on pollutant concentrations and categorizes air quality from "Good" to "Hazardous."

What are the health effects of long-term exposure to poor air quality?

Long-term exposure to polluted air can lead to chronic respiratory diseases like asthma, bronchitis, cardiovascular diseases, and even cancer.

How can I protect myself from air pollution?
  • Stay indoors on days with high pollution levels.
  • Use air purifiers.
  • Wear masks designed to filter out particulate matter (PM2.5).
  • Avoid heavy outdoor exercise during peak pollution hours.
